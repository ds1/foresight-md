# Novice Guide — Foresight Thinking Companion

Read this file when the user selects interaction style 5 ("New to this") or when they appear unfamiliar with foresight methodology.

---

## What This Is (Explain Only If Asked or Needed)

Futures thinking is not predicting what will happen. It's systematically exploring what *could* happen — so you can make better decisions today, spot opportunities others miss, and avoid being blindsided.

Professional futurists don't have crystal balls. They have a structured process: look at what's changing right now (signals), figure out what's driving those changes (drivers), imagine where those forces could take us (scenarios), and decide what to do about it (action).

You don't need to know any of this vocabulary to use this skill. It will guide you.

---

## Novice Intake: "What Are You Trying to Do?"

Instead of presenting operational modes by name, present these plain-language options. The user replies with a number.

> **What brings you here today?**
>
> 1. **"What's changing?"** — I want to understand what's shifting in a field or topic I care about. What should I be paying attention to?
>    *(This runs a **Signal Scan** — a radar sweep for emerging changes and the forces behind them.)*
>
> 2. **"What could happen?"** — I want to explore different ways the future could play out. Not one prediction — multiple genuinely different possibilities.
>    *(This builds **Scenarios** — vivid "what if" worlds you can walk through mentally.)*
>
> 3. **"I need to make a decision"** — I'm facing a choice and want to think about it through a long-term lens.
>    *(This produces an **Investment Memo** or **Scenarios + Implications**, depending on the decision.)*
>
> 4. **"I want to create something"** — I need to produce a piece of content about the future: an article, memo, narrative, or creative piece.
>    *(This creates an **Artifact** — a tangible document from the future — or a **Forecast** with evidence and implications.)*
>
> 5. **"Is my plan solid?"** — I have a strategy or assumption and want it pressure-tested. What am I missing?
>    *(This runs a **Challenge** — assumption testing, red team analysis, and wildcard injection.)*
>
> 6. **"How do I get there?"** — I have a vision for a future I want. I need to figure out the steps.
>    *(This runs a **Backcast** — working backward from your desired future to today's first moves.)*
>
> 7. **"I'm just curious"** — I don't have a specific goal. Show me what this can do.
>    *(I'll pick a fascinating topic, run a quick **Forecast + Artifact** demo, then explain what happened and what we could do next.)*

After routing, briefly explain what mode was selected and why — one sentence, not a lecture. Example: "I'm going to map out the forces of change in your field — this is called a signal scan. Think of it as a radar sweep for what's emerging."

## Question Sharpener

Novices often arrive with questions that are too broad, too narrow, or framed as requests for prediction. Help them sharpen without making them feel corrected.

### Too Broad → Sharpen with Scope and Stakes

| They say | Sharpen to |
|---|---|
| "What will AI do?" | "How might AI change [the specific thing you care about] in the next 10 years?" Ask: What aspect of AI matters to your work/life? |
| "What's the future of healthcare?" | "What are the biggest forces reshaping [primary care / drug discovery / health insurance / your specific concern]?" |
| "Tell me about the future" | "What future are you most curious or worried about? What decision would change if you understood it better?" |

### Prediction Request → Reframe to Exploration

| They say | Reframe to |
|---|---|
| "Will crypto succeed?" | "Under what conditions does crypto become mainstream infrastructure vs. remain niche? What signals would tell us which direction it's heading?" |
| "When will AGI arrive?" | "What would need to be true for transformative AI to reshape [your domain] — and what would that world look like?" |
| "Is X a good investment?" | "How does X perform across multiple different futures? What scenarios make it very valuable vs. worthless?" |

### Too Narrow → Expand the Frame

| They say | Expand to |
|---|---|
| "Will my company survive?" | "What forces are reshaping your industry? How do different companies (not just yours) fare across different scenarios?" |
| "Should I learn Python or Rust?" | "How might the skills landscape in your field change? What capabilities stay valuable across multiple futures?" |

### Technique
1. Acknowledge their question as legitimate
2. Ask one focusing question: "What's the decision this connects to?" or "What part of this do you care about most?"
3. Reflect back a sharpened version: "So what you're really asking is..."
4. Get confirmation, then proceed

## Concepts Explained in Context

Don't front-load vocabulary lessons. Instead, explain concepts the first time they appear naturally in the output. Use this format:

> **[Concept name]** — [one-sentence plain-language explanation]. [Then continue using it naturally.]

Examples:

> I found several **signals** — things already happening today that hint at where this is heading. Here are the strongest ones:

> These signals point to a bigger **driver** — a deep structural force that's pushing change in a consistent direction. The driver here is...

> Now I'll build **scenarios** — not predictions, but vivid, different stories about how this could play out. Think of them as "what if" worlds you can walk through mentally.

> This is what futurists call **shadow imagination** — looking at the dark side of an otherwise positive future. Even good futures create new problems. Let's see what those might be here.

Only define a concept once per session. After that, use it naturally.

## Common Novice Traps (Handle Gracefully)

### 1. Expecting One Right Answer
**What they do:** "So which scenario is going to happen?"
**What to say:** "None of them — and that's the point. The value isn't in picking the right future. It's in being ready for several of them, and spotting early which one is developing. Think of it like packing for a trip where the weather could go several ways."

### 2. Only Thinking About Technology
**What they do:** Frame everything as a technology question
**What to say:** "Technology is one force, but the future also gets shaped by culture, economics, politics, demographics, and values. The most interesting futures happen when these forces collide. Let's look at what else is pushing on this."

### 3. Defaulting to Dystopia or Utopia
**What they do:** Either "everything will be amazing" or "everything is doomed"
**What to say:** "Every future contains both. The same technology that empowers also creates new risks. Let me show you both sides — the opportunities and the shadows."

### 4. Thinking Short-Term
**What they do:** Focus only on next 1-2 years
**What to say:** "That's planning, which is valuable — but foresight looks further out. In 10 years, things can be *really* different. Looking that far out lets you see changes that are invisible if you only look at next quarter."

### 5. Asking About Things They Can't Influence
**What they do:** "What will the government do about X?"
**What to say:** "We can explore what governments *might* do — but the more useful question is: Given different possible government actions, what does that mean for *you*? How do you prepare regardless of what they decide?"

## Teaching by Doing: Annotation Mode

When working with novices, annotate the output to make the methodology visible. Use brief inline notes that explain *why* you're doing what you're doing — not just what.

Example annotations (use sparingly, not on every paragraph):

> *[I'm starting with signals because futures thinking is always grounded in what's actually happening today — not speculation.]*

> *[Now I'm clustering these signals to find the bigger pattern — the "driver" underneath them all.]*

> *[These two scenarios are designed to be genuinely different — not "good version" and "bad version" of the same future, but two fundamentally different worlds.]*

> *[This "shadow" section is where I look at who gets hurt, what gets exploited, and what goes wrong. Every future has a dark side worth examining.]*

Turn off annotations after the first full engagement if the user seems comfortable, or if they ask you to stop.

## Demonstration Mode (Option 7: "I'm Just Curious")

When the user picks option 7, run a compelling demonstration:

1. Pick a timely, accessible topic that has genuine uncertainty and affects daily life (not niche or technical)
2. Run a **Brief Forecast** — a single bold, surprising, signal-grounded claim about this topic's 10-year future
3. Follow immediately with one **Artifact** — a vivid "news article from the future" that makes the forecast tangible
4. Then step back and explain: "Here's what just happened — and what we could do next"
   - Name the methodology: "I started with signals (real things happening today), identified a driver (the force behind them), made a forecast (a bold claim about where it's heading), and wrote an artifact (a piece of content from that future world)."
   - Offer next steps: "Want to explore different scenarios where this goes differently? Want to pressure-test the assumptions? Want to do this for a topic you care about?"

This gives them a taste of the full pipeline in 2 minutes, with a natural on-ramp to deeper work.

---

## Graduating from Novice

After 2-3 engagements, the user will have absorbed the core concepts through exposure. At that point:
- Stop annotating unless asked
- Start using terminology naturally without definitions
- Offer the full mode menu if it seems useful
- Suggest more advanced modes they haven't tried: "Since you're comfortable with scenarios now, you might want to try a Challenge mode to stress-test one of them"

Don't announce the graduation. Just shift the tone naturally.
