# Scenario Development Reference

## What Is a Scenario?

A scenario is a specific, internally consistent story set in a future that could plausibly happen. It describes what you might see, feel, and experience if you woke up in that future. It is not a prediction — it is a thinking tool.

Opening frame: **"This is a world in which..."** — the futurist's version of "once upon a time."

A scenario can take any narrative form: short story, news article, documentary script, diary entry, product page, policy brief, legal ruling, job listing, social media thread.

---

## The 2x2 Scenario Matrix Method

The standard approach for generating genuinely divergent scenarios:

### Step 1: Frame the Question
Define the domain and time horizon: "The future of [X] in [Y] years."

### Step 2: Identify Critical Uncertainties
From your signal scan and driver analysis, identify the 2 most impactful uncertainties — things that could go either way and would fundamentally change the landscape.

Good uncertainty axes:
- Must be genuinely uncertain (not a settled trend)
- Must be high-impact (the future looks very different depending on which way it goes)
- Must be independent of each other (not correlated)

### Step 3: Build the Matrix
Cross the two uncertainties to create 4 quadrants. Each quadrant is a distinct scenario world.

```
                    Uncertainty A: [one extreme]
                           |
    Scenario 2             |           Scenario 3
    [Name]                 |           [Name]
                           |
Uncertainty B: ————————————+———————————— Uncertainty B:
[one extreme]              |              [other extreme]
                           |
    Scenario 1             |           Scenario 4
    [Name]                 |           [Name]
                           |
                    Uncertainty A: [other extreme]
```

### Step 4: Develop Each Scenario
For each quadrant, build out using the Vian Questions (below).

### Step 5: Extract Cross-Scenario Insights
What's true across all four? What actions are robust regardless of which future arrives? These are the strategic gold.

## Kathi Vian's Scenario Development Questions

Use these to build depth and rigor into each scenario:

1. **Day-in-the-life:** What does a typical day look like for different people in this world? What do they see first thing in the morning? What do they worry about? What makes them happy?

2. **New structures:** What new institutions, organizations, processes, laws, or concepts are needed to support this future? What old ones have been replaced or transformed?

3. **Catalysts:** Who or what are the actors that made this future happen? Which companies, movements, governments, technologies, or events were the catalysts?

4. **Geographies:** Is this future universal or does it manifest differently in different parts of the world? Who's ahead, who's behind, and why?

5. **Historical precedents:** What past transformations most closely parallel this scenario? What do those precedents teach us about critical dilemmas, timelines, and unintended consequences?

6. **Sustainability:** Could this scenario last 100+ years? What makes it sustainable or unsustainable? What internal contradictions might cause it to evolve or collapse?

7. **The flip:** What's the utopian version of this scenario? The dystopian version? Every scenario contains seeds of both.

## Positive and Shadow Imagination

Every future contains both:

**Positive imagination:** What could go wonderfully right? What new capabilities, freedoms, connections, or opportunities emerge? What problems get solved? Who is newly empowered?

**Shadow imagination:** What new problems, complications, exploits, and harms could emerge? Who loses power, access, or safety? What gets weaponized, monopolized, or corrupted? What unintended consequences cascade?

A scenario that only contains positive imagination is naive. A scenario that only contains shadow imagination is paralyzing. Rigorous foresight always develops both, in every scenario.

### Shadow Probing Questions
- Who could exploit this future? What would they game, cheat, or take advantage of?
- Who is at a disadvantage in this world? Whose situation gets worse?
- What new inequalities emerge?
- What emotions dominate — and what do people do when they feel that way?
- What are people afraid of that they weren't afraid of before?
- What old problems come back in new forms?

## Moving Scenarios (Marchais-Roubelat & Roubelat)

An extension of traditional scenario planning that rejects fixed "end-states":

- Scenarios should not be static snapshots but **ongoing transformations** with their own internal dynamics
- Include shifting stakeholder dominance over time
- Allow scenarios to **distort and transform** — a scenario that starts in one quadrant may drift toward another
- Model the transition dynamics, not just the destination
- Consider: What happens when actors in the scenario react to the scenario itself? (Reflexivity)

Use this approach for domains with high complexity and long time horizons where "end-state" thinking is misleading.

## Scenario Writing Conventions

- Write in **present tense** — "In this world, people wake up to..." not "people would wake up to..."
- Include **concrete, lived details** — names, places, numbers, textures, sounds, daily routines
- Address **multiple dimensions of life:** personal, relational, cultural, institutional, economic, political
- Ground each scenario in **specific signals from today** — the reader should be able to trace the causal chain
- Balance **positive and shadow imagination** in every scenario
- Make the scenario **surprising but plausible** — if it's obvious, it's not doing its job
- End with **implications and questions**, not conclusions — scenarios should open thinking, not close it

### Scenario Quality Check
Before finalizing any scenario, verify:
- [ ] Can I trace a credible causal path from today's signals through known drivers to this world?
- [ ] Is this genuinely different from the other scenarios in the set (not a variation on the same theme)?
- [ ] Does it contain both positive and shadow elements?
- [ ] Are there concrete day-in-the-life details?
- [ ] Would someone reading this feel something — hope, fear, curiosity, urgency?
- [ ] Does it raise more questions than it answers?
