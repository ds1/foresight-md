# Analytical Techniques Reference

## Backcasting

Work backward from a desired future to identify present-day actions.

### Process
1. **Define the desired end-state** in vivid, concrete terms. Not vague ("a better world") but specific ("a world in which X is true, Y has changed, and Z is the norm").
2. **Identify necessary preconditions** — What must be true for this future to exist? Work backward: What had to happen in year 9? Year 7? Year 5? Year 3? Year 1?
3. **Map the critical path** — Which milestones are sequential (must happen in order) vs. parallel (can happen simultaneously)?
4. **Identify leverage points** — Where can small actions create disproportionate movement toward the desired future? Look for: regulatory windows, technology inflection points, cultural tipping points, funding cycles.
5. **Name the first moves** — What can be done this week? This month? This quarter? Make it concrete and actionable.
6. **Anticipate resistance** — What forces, actors, or structures will push back? How do you navigate them — go through, go around, or convert them?

### When to Use
- You have a clear vision of a desirable future but no roadmap
- Strategic planning that needs to be vision-led rather than trend-extrapolated
- Identifying the gap between where trends are heading and where you want to go

## Pre-Mortem

Identify failure modes before they happen by imagining the project/strategy has already failed.

### Process
1. Set the frame: "It's [time horizon] from now. This initiative has failed completely. It's a disaster."
2. Each participant independently writes down: "What happened? Why did it fail?"
3. Share and cluster the failure reasons.
4. For each cluster, assess: How likely is this? How preventable? What early warning signals would we see?
5. Design mitigations or pivot triggers for the most likely and most damaging failure modes.

### Why It Works
- Overcomes optimism bias and groupthink
- Gives permission to voice concerns that feel disloyal in a forward-looking planning session
- Prospective hindsight is psychologically easier than pure speculation about risk

## Red Team / Devil's Advocacy

Argue the strongest possible case against a position, forecast, or strategy.

### Process
1. State the position clearly.
2. Assign (or adopt) the role of the adversary — someone who genuinely believes this position is wrong, dangerous, or naive.
3. Build the strongest possible counter-argument using:
   - Counter-signals (evidence pointing the other direction)
   - Historical analogies where similar bets failed
   - Structural barriers the original position underweights
   - Stakeholder opposition that could block or corrupt implementation
   - Unintended consequences the original position ignores
4. Present the counter-argument as if you believe it.
5. Evaluate: Which counter-arguments are strongest? Do they require modifying the original position?

### Rules
- The red team must be genuinely adversarial, not a softball exercise
- Steelman the opposition — argue their best case, not their weakest
- Separate the exercise from personal commitment to any position

## Assumption Surfacing and Testing

Make implicit beliefs explicit, then stress-test them.

### Process
1. **Extract assumptions** from the user's framing, strategy, or forecast. Listen for:
   - "Obviously..." / "Of course..." / "Everyone knows..."
   - Unstated premises that the argument depends on
   - Beliefs about how systems work, what people want, what's possible
2. **Classify each assumption:**
   - **Load-bearing:** If wrong, the entire argument collapses
   - **Adjustable:** If wrong, the argument needs modification but survives
   - **Cosmetic:** If wrong, nothing important changes
3. **Test load-bearing assumptions:**
   - What evidence supports this? How strong is that evidence?
   - What evidence contradicts this? How are you weighting it?
   - What would you expect to see if this assumption were wrong?
   - Has this assumption been true historically? Under what conditions did it break?
4. **Inversion test:** For each load-bearing assumption, ask: "What if the opposite were true? What would the world look like?"

## Implication Mapping

Map the cascading consequences of a specific change.

### Process
1. **State the change** clearly and specifically.
2. **First-order effects:** Direct, obvious, immediate consequences. "If X, then Y."
3. **Second-order effects:** Consequences of the consequences. "If Y, then Z." These are often where the interesting strategic opportunities live.
4. **Third-order effects:** The surprising, non-obvious ripples. Often cross domain boundaries. "If Z in healthcare, then W in education."
5. **Shadow implications:** How could this be exploited? What new inequalities, harms, or perverse incentives emerge?
6. **Adaptive responses:** How will people, institutions, and markets adapt? What new behaviors, products, regulations, or social norms develop in response?
7. **Opportunity zones:** Where does value get created as a result of this cascade?

### Tips
- Force yourself past the obvious first-order effects. The value is in 2nd and 3rd order.
- Cross domain boundaries deliberately: "What does this mean for education? Healthcare? Governance? Culture? Crime?"
- Consider different actor perspectives: individuals, corporations, governments, civil society, criminals

## Historical Pattern Analysis (Look Back to Look Forward)

### Process
1. **Define the topic and forward horizon** (e.g., "the future of credentialing, 10 years out").
2. **Set the backward horizon** at 2x the forward horizon minimum (20+ years back).
3. **Build the timeline** — key moments when change happened: events, laws, inventions, breakdowns, shifts.
4. **Identify patterns:**
   - What cycles repeat? (Boom-bust, centralize-decentralize, regulate-deregulate)
   - What accelerates over time? (Adoption curves, cost declines, capability improvements)
   - What structural shifts echo across eras? (Printing press → internet, enclosure → platforms)
5. **Apply Amara's Law:** "We tend to overestimate the effect of a technology in the short run and underestimate the effect in the long run." Where might this apply to the current moment?
6. **Extract principles:** What do the patterns teach about how change actually happens in this domain?
7. **Project forward:** How might these patterns continue? Where do they suggest inflection points?
8. **Caveat the limits:** Where does the historical analogy break down? What's genuinely unprecedented about the current moment?

## Wildcard Injection

Introduce plausible disruptions that haven't been considered.

### Process
1. Identify the domain's "assumed stabilities" — things everyone takes for granted will continue.
2. For each stability, ask: "What event, development, or shift could break this assumption?"
3. Evaluate each wildcard on:
   - **Plausibility:** Not likely, but not impossible. Could happen.
   - **Impact:** If it happened, how much would it change the landscape?
   - **Speed:** How quickly would effects propagate?
4. Select 2-3 highest-impact wildcards and briefly sketch what the domain looks like afterward.
5. Ask: "Are we prepared for this? What would we need to do differently if this were on the table?"

### Types of Wildcards
- Technological breakthrough (or catastrophic failure)
- Geopolitical shock (conflict, alliance shift, regime change)
- Environmental discontinuity (climate tipping point, pandemic, resource shock)
- Social/cultural sudden shift (movement, scandal, norm collapse)
- Regulatory earthquake (landmark ruling, treaty, ban)
- Black swan financial event (currency collapse, market structure failure)
