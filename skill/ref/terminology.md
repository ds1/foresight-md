# Foresight Terminology Reference

## Core Concepts

**Signal of Change**
Something happening today that could be a clue to the future. Present-day evidence — a new technology, behavior, law, breakdown, product, crime, research result, or data point. The empirical foundation of foresight. Must be observable and sourceable.

**Driver / Future Force**
A macro-level force that moves society toward particular futures. Persistent and broad — not a single event but an ongoing structural shift. Examples: aging demographics, platform economics, climate disruption, AI capability growth. Behind every signal is at least one driver.

**Forecast**
A prediction, based on data and evidence from today, about what might happen in the future. Forecasts are evidence-based, bold, and held lightly. Multiple competing forecasts are better than one "most likely" forecast.

**Scenario**
An internally consistent, plausible story about a specific alternative future. Not a prediction but a thinking tool. Describes what you'd see, feel, and experience in that future. Written in present tense. Grounded in signals and drivers.

**Artifact from the Future**
A tangible piece of content (news article, product page, policy brief, etc.) designed as if it already exists in a future world. Makes the abstract concrete. Always includes a "back-link" to present-day signals.

## Temporal Concepts

**Time Horizon**
The forward distance of a foresight exercise. IFTF's sweet spot is 10 years — far enough for things to be genuinely different, close enough to feel real and actionable. Shorter horizons (2-5 years) are more constrained by present conditions. Longer horizons (20-50 years) require more speculation.

**Amara's Law**
"We tend to overestimate the effect of a technology in the short run and underestimate the effect in the long run." — Roy Amara, past president of IFTF. Critical for technology forecasting and investment timing.

**Backcasting**
Working backward from a desired future to identify present-day actions. The reverse of forecasting (which projects forward from present trends). Used for strategic planning toward a specific goal.

## Futures Typology

**Cone of Plausibility (Hancock & Bezold)**
The expanding possibility space from present to future:
- **Probable:** Most likely if current trends continue. Useful for budgeting, dangerous for strategy.
- **Plausible:** Could happen given known drivers and reasonable extrapolation. The primary working space.
- **Possible:** Might happen including low-probability events, breakthroughs, and wildcards. Used for stress-testing.
- **Preferable:** What we want to happen. The normative/values dimension. Used to orient action.

**Monofuturism**
The (rejected) assumption that there is one single future to be discovered or predicted. All rigorous foresight works with multiple futures.

**Wildcard**
A low-probability, high-impact event that would fundamentally change the landscape if it occurred. Not a prediction. Used for stress-testing strategies and expanding imagination.

**Weak Signal**
A signal that is very early, ambiguous, and easy to dismiss. May indicate a major shift or may be noise. Worth tracking but not building strategies on until confirmed by additional signals.

**Megatrend**
A large, slow-moving, deeply structural force of change that shapes the broad context for decades. Examples: urbanization, population aging, digitization, climate change. Megatrends set the stage; signals and drivers operate within and against them.

## Analytical Concepts

**Two-Curve Framework (Morrison/IFTF)**
In transformational periods, society lives along two simultaneous curves: the declining first curve (today's way) and the emerging second curve (tomorrow's way). Strategy must operate on both.

**STEEP+V Categories**
A checklist for comprehensive environmental scanning:
- **S**ocial — demographics, culture, lifestyle, values, inequality
- **T**echnological — capabilities, platforms, infrastructure, automation
- **E**conomic — markets, labor, trade, business models, financialization
- **E**nvironmental — climate, resources, biodiversity, energy
- **P**olitical — governance, regulation, geopolitics, institutional trust
- **V**alues — ethics, generational shifts, rights, secular/religious dynamics

**Positive Imagination / Shadow Imagination**
Two complementary lenses for exploring any future. Positive imagination: what could go wonderfully right. Shadow imagination: what new problems, exploits, and harms could emerge. Rigorous foresight always applies both.

**Cascio Pentagon**
Five-dimensional quality evaluation for foresight outputs: Logical, Complex/Nuanced, Evocative, Provocative, Stimulating.

**F-I-A Cycle**
Foresight → Insight → Action. The core workflow of applied foresight. Foresight generates understanding, insight translates it for a specific context, action makes it operational.

## Cognitive Concepts

**Future Shock (Toffler)**
Disorientation and malaise caused by rapid change exceeding people's ability to adapt. The motivation for foresight work — systematic thinking about the future reduces future shock.

**Urgent Optimism (McGonigal)**
Confidence that the future can be better combined with the urgency to act now. Not naive optimism (everything will be fine) but active optimism (we can make it better if we act).

**Polak's Two Axes**
Orientation toward the future measured on optimism (better/worse) and agency (powerful/powerless). The goal of foresight: increase agency regardless of optimism level.

**Futures Metacognition (Hejazi)**
Awareness and regulation of one's own futures thinking processes. The ability to monitor your biases, assumptions, and mental models while thinking about the future. The highest-order futures thinking skill.

**Postformal Thinking (Gidley)**
Higher-order reasoning beyond logical/analytical thinking. Features: complexity, contextualization, creativity, dialectics, imagination, paradox tolerance, reflexivity. The cognitive mode most suited to navigating genuine uncertainty.

## Practice Concepts

**Sensing Organization (Gorbis)**
An organization that systematically collects, shares, and interprets signals of change as a regular practice, not a one-time exercise.

**Strong Opinions, Lightly Held (Saffo)**
Make bold forecasts that express a clear point of view. Then actively seek evidence against your position and be willing to update. The opposite of both timidity and dogmatism.

**Provotype (Matos-Castaño et al.)**
A provocative prototype — a tangible artifact designed not to demonstrate feasibility but to challenge assumptions and provoke discussion about values and priorities.

**T-Shaped Thinker**
A person with depth in one domain and breadth across many. The ideal profile for futures work, which requires synthesizing signals from technology, culture, economics, politics, and more.
