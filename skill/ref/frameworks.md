# Foresight Frameworks Reference

## F-I-A Cycle (IFTF Core)

The foundational cycle for all foresight work:

1. **Foresight** — Develop a plausible, internally consistent view of the future (typically 5-10 year horizon). Built from signals, drivers, and scenarios.
2. **Insight** — Ask "What does this mean for us?" The same foresight yields different insights depending on the actor's position, industry, values, and goals.
3. **Action** — Identify what to do today or near-term to prepare for or shape that future. The foresight may be 10 years out; the action may be needed this quarter.

The cycle repeats. New signals feed new foresight. New actions generate new signals.

## Two-Curve Framework (Ian Morrison / IFTF)

In any period of large transformation, society lives along two curves simultaneously:

- **First Curve (declining):** Today's way of doing things. Established rules, regulations, usage patterns, institutions. Slowly declining, though the exact angle is unknown.
- **Second Curve (emerging):** Tomorrow's way of doing things. Nascent, visible mainly through early signals. New rules, new structures, new power dynamics.

The most interesting strategic space is the overlap zone where both curves coexist. Organizations must operate on the first curve while investing in the second.

Application: When analyzing any domain, identify what's on each curve. What's declining? What's emerging? Where is the crossover point?

## Cone of Plausibility (Hancock & Bezold)

A visual model of the expanding possibility space from the present into the future:

- **Probable** (narrow center): What's most likely if current trends continue. Useful for planning, dangerous for strategy.
- **Plausible** (wider): What could happen given known drivers and reasonable extrapolation. The working space for most foresight.
- **Possible** (widest): What might happen including low-probability events, breakthroughs, and wildcards. The space for stress-testing.
- **Preferable** (overlay): What we want to happen. Not a prediction but a target for action. The normative dimension.

Good foresight works primarily in the plausible zone, ventures into the possible for stress-testing, and uses the preferable to orient action.

## Stanford Foresight Framework (5 Phases)

From the Playbook for Strategic Foresight and Innovation (Carleton & Cockayne):

### Phase 1: Perspective
Broaden the view of the problem space.
- **Context Map:** Map the ecosystem of forces, actors, and trends surrounding the domain.
- **Progression Curves:** Track how key capabilities, costs, or behaviors have evolved over time. Extrapolate trajectories.
- **Janus Cones:** Look simultaneously backward and forward — what patterns from the past project into which futures?

### Phase 2: Opportunity
Identify future customers, needs, and markets.
- **Generational Arcs:** How do different generations experience and shape the domain differently?
- **Future User:** Build detailed profiles of people who will live in the future world.
- **Futuretelling:** Narrative technique for making future opportunities vivid and shareable.

### Phase 3: Solution
Turn ideas into tangible artifacts and plans.
- **White Spots:** Identify gaps in the current landscape that no one is filling.
- **Paper Mockups:** Rapid prototyping of future products, services, or experiences.
- **Change Paths:** Map the implementation journey from present to future state.

### Phase 4: Team
Find and organize the right people for the work.
- **Buddy Checks:** Pair diverse thinkers to cross-pollinate perspectives.
- **VOICE Stars:** Map stakeholders by their Value, Opinions, Interests, Concerns, and Expectations.

### Phase 5: Vision
Set direction and test ambition.
- **Vision Statement:** A clear, compelling description of the desired future state.
- **DARPA Hard Test:** Is the vision ambitious enough to be transformative? Would DARPA fund it?
- **Pathfinders:** Identify early adopters and pioneers already moving toward this future.

## Five Families of Structured Analytic Techniques (Pherson/Donner/Gnad)

### 1. Exploration Techniques
Discover what you don't know. Open up the problem space.
- Brainstorming variants, mind mapping, cluster analysis, key assumptions check

### 2. Diagnostic Techniques
Evaluate hypotheses and weigh evidence.
- Analysis of competing hypotheses, weighted ranking, consistency checks

### 3. Reframing Techniques
Challenge assumptions and shift mental models.
- Red team analysis, devil's advocacy, structured self-critique, outside-in thinking, premortem

### 4. Foresight Techniques
Explore alternative futures systematically.
- Scenario planning, indicator development, driver mapping, trend impact analysis, cross-impact analysis

### 5. Decision Support Techniques
Translate analysis into actionable options.
- Decision matrices, opportunity analysis, risk assessment, implementation roadmaps

## Polak's Two-Axis Model (Fred Polak / IFTF)

Assess any group's or individual's orientation toward the future along two dimensions:

- **X-axis: Optimism ↔ Pessimism** — Is the future getting better or worse?
- **Y-axis: Agency ↔ Powerlessness** — How much power do you have to shape it?

Four quadrants:
| | Getting Better | Getting Worse |
|---|---|---|
| **High Agency** | Gaming skills (accelerate positive change, watch for blind spots) | Action skills (urgent optimism, strategy, coalition-building) |
| **Low Agency** | Forecasting skills (find entry points for engagement) | Simulation skills (find reasons for hope, identify concrete roles) |

The goal of foresight work is to move people toward higher agency regardless of their optimism level.

## Futures Metacognition Pyramid (Hejazi)

Three layers of how humans engage with the future:

1. **Pre-cognition** — Intuitive, visceral sensing. Gut feelings about what's coming. Highly visual and embodied.
2. **Cognition** — Deliberate information processing about the future. Analysis, extrapolation, modeling.
3. **Metacognition** — Monitoring and controlling one's own futures thinking. Awareness of biases, assumptions, and mental models. The most sophisticated and trainable layer.

Key insight: Futures thinking is not purely analytical — it is deeply visual and embodied. People "see" the future rather than just thinking about it. Effective foresight work engages all three layers.

## Responsible Futuring Framework (Matos-Castaño et al.)

Four cognitive/experiential capacities:

1. **Understanding Values** — Surface the diversity of contested values in complex challenges. What do different stakeholders actually care about?
2. **Imagining** — Forward-thinking, performative capacity. Connect imagination to empathy. What would it feel like to live in this future?
3. **Tangibilizing** — Make futures concrete through "provotypes" (provocative prototypes) and artifacts. Move from abstract to tangible.
4. **Introspecting** — Backcasting, reflexivity, evaluating your own assumptions. What are you taking for granted?

Five process phases: (0) Setting the Stage → (1) Connect and Relate → (2) Explore and Challenge → (3) Make and Envision → (4) Reflect and Reframe
