# Content Production Reference

## Artifacts from the Future

An artifact from the future is a tangible piece of content written as if it already exists in a future world. It makes the abstract future concrete and experiential.

### Artifact Types
- **News article or op-ed** from a future publication
- **Product page or advertisement** for a future product/service
- **Policy brief or government announcement**
- **Investment memo or earnings call transcript**
- **Personal diary entry, letter, or social media post**
- **Job listing or university course catalogue**
- **Legal document, regulatory filing, or court ruling**
- **Patent application or research abstract**
- **Restaurant menu, real estate listing, or insurance policy**
- **Interview transcript or oral history**

### Artifact Design Principles
1. **Write in present tense** — the future is now. "The new policy requires..." not "The policy would require..."
2. **Ground in specific signals** — every artifact should trace back to something happening today
3. **Include concrete, lived details** — names, places, numbers, dates, textures, brands, slang. Specificity creates believability.
4. **Serve as a thinking tool** — the artifact should provoke "What would I do in this world?" and "Do I want this?"
5. **Include both positive and shadow elements** — even a product ad implies a world with both benefits and costs
6. **Match the genre conventions** — a news article should read like a news article, not like a scenario summary

### Artifact Appendix
Every artifact should include a brief appendix (separate from the artifact itself) that:
- Names the scenario world it belongs to
- Lists 3-5 present-day signals that ground it
- Identifies the key drivers
- Notes what's positive and what's shadowed in this future

## Science Fiction Prototyping

A methodology (from Brian David Johnson / Intel) for using fiction to explore futures:

- Short narratives (1,000-10,000 words) based on scientific or technological fact
- Set in the near future (5-15 years)
- Center on a human character experiencing the consequences of a specific change
- Explore the implications — especially the unintended, messy, human ones
- Include both the light and the dark
- End with questions, not resolutions

### When to Use
- When abstract analysis isn't landing with the audience
- When you need to make consequences emotionally real
- When exploring ethical dimensions of emerging technology
- When the audience includes non-specialists who need narrative entry points

## Forecast Writing Structure

For a standalone forecast document:

1. **Headline:** A provocative, one-sentence claim about the future. Bold, surprising, specific.
2. **Signal evidence:** 3-5 present-day signals with sources. Concrete, verifiable, dated.
3. **Driving forces:** The macro-drivers creating momentum toward this future. Named and explained.
4. **Implications cascade:** 2nd and 3rd-order consequences for individuals, organizations, markets, societies.
5. **Shadow side:** What could go wrong, who loses, what gets exploited, what new problems emerge.
6. **Confidence and contingency:** What conditions would make this more likely? Less likely? What would you need to see to update your confidence?
7. **Action implications:** Given this forecast, what should the reader consider doing differently?
8. **Self-evaluation:** Brief Cascio Pentagon score.

## Investment Memo Structure (Futures Lens)

1. **Thesis:** One-paragraph statement of the opportunity and why it matters on a 5-10 year horizon.
2. **Landscape scan:** Key signals and drivers shaping the relevant domain. What's changing and why.
3. **Two-Curve positioning:** Where does this opportunity sit? First curve (incumbent, declining)? Second curve (emerging, ascending)? Transition zone?
4. **Scenario exposure:** How does this opportunity perform across 3-4 divergent futures? Robust across scenarios, or a concentrated bet on one?
5. **Second-order market creation:** What new markets, behaviors, or platforms does this enable or destroy?
6. **Timing analysis:** Is this ahead of, behind, or on the adoption curve? Use Amara's Law: are we overestimating short-term impact or underestimating long-term?
7. **Risk through futures lens:** What scenarios make this worthless? What scenarios make it 100x? What's the ratio?
8. **Strategic optionality:** Does this investment increase or decrease the investor's future flexibility?
9. **Action:** Buy/build/watch/avoid, with conditions for revisiting.

## Audience Calibration

Different audiences need different output:

| Audience | Emphasis | Tone | Length |
|----------|----------|------|--------|
| Executive / board | Action implications, strategic options, risk | Direct, decisive, quantified where possible | Short (1-2 pages) |
| Strategy team | Full analysis, scenarios, evidence chain | Analytical, structured, open to debate | Medium (5-15 pages) |
| Innovation team | Opportunities, white spaces, provocations | Energizing, creative, possibility-focused | Variable |
| Investment committee | Thesis, scenario exposure, timing, risk | Rigorous, evidence-heavy, probability-aware | Structured memo (3-5 pages) |
| General public / newsletter | Narrative, surprise, concrete details | Accessible, vivid, emotionally engaging | Short (800-2000 words) |
| Workshop participants | Interactive prompts, exercises, discussion starters | Facilitative, question-rich, open | Modular |

## Writing Conventions

- **Lead with the bold claim**, then provide evidence. Don't bury the insight.
- **Use concrete specifics** over abstractions. "A farmer in Kerala using satellite soil analysis" beats "agricultural technology adoption in developing markets."
- **Write scenarios in present tense.** "In this world, people wake up to..." not "people would wake up to..."
- **Mark speculation explicitly** but don't apologize for it. "A speculative but signal-grounded possibility:" is honest framing. "I'm just guessing here" undermines the work.
- **Include "So What?" sections** that translate foresight into actionable insight. Never leave the reader with only description.
- **End with questions, not conclusions** when the goal is to open thinking. End with recommendations when the goal is to drive action.
- **Name your uncertainty.** Use confidence language: "The signals strongly suggest..." vs. "A speculative but plausible possibility is..." vs. "If [condition], then..."
