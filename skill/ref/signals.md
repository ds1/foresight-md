# Signals & Drivers Reference

## What Is a Signal?

A **signal of change** is anything happening today that could be a clue to how things might be different in the future. It is present-day evidence — not speculation.

A signal might be:
- A new technology, product, or service
- A first successful demonstration or a first major breakdown
- A new law, regulation, or legal precedent
- A new kind of crime, conflict, or social behavior
- A research breakthrough or prototype
- A surprising data point or statistical shift
- An anecdote or observation that makes you say: "That's new. That's weird. Why is this happening?"

William Gibson: "The future is already here — it's just not very evenly distributed." Signals are where the future is already showing up.

---

## Signal Analysis Process

For each signal, work through:

### 1. Description
What is actually happening? Be specific, concrete, and source it.

### 2. Direction of Change
What kind of change does this signal represent? From what now → to what next?
- Example: From centralized → to distributed. From human-performed → to automated. From owned → to accessed.

### 3. Driver Identification
What future force is creating this change? What trend, macro-phenomenon, or structural shift is driving it?
- Ask: "Why is this happening now? What larger force made this possible or necessary?"

### 4. Signal Strength Rating
- **Early:** First occurrence, experimental, limited to one context. May not go anywhere.
- **Emerging:** Multiple independent occurrences, starting to attract attention and investment.
- **Accelerating:** Growing rapidly, institutional adoption beginning, counter-signals appearing.
- **Mainstream:** Widely adopted, becoming the new normal, regulatory frameworks forming.

### 5. Signal Clustering
Group related signals to reveal underlying patterns. Three or more signals pointing in the same direction indicate a driver worth building forecasts around.

## From Signals to Drivers

**Drivers** (also called "future forces") are the macro-level forces that move us toward particular futures. Behind every signal, there is at least one driver. Drivers are not events — they are persistent forces.

Categories for scanning (STEEP+V):
- **Social:** Demographics, cultural shifts, lifestyle changes, values evolution, inequality dynamics
- **Technological:** New capabilities, platform shifts, infrastructure changes, automation, biotech, energy
- **Economic:** Market structures, labor markets, trade patterns, financialization, inequality, new business models
- **Environmental:** Climate change, resource depletion, biodiversity, pollution, extreme weather, energy transition
- **Political:** Governance models, regulation, geopolitics, institutional trust, democratic erosion, sovereignty
- **Values:** Ethical frameworks, generational values, religious/secular shifts, rights expansion/contraction

### Driver Documentation Template
```
Driver: [Name — short, descriptive]
Direction: From [current state] → To [future state]
Strength: [Weak / Moderate / Strong / Dominant]
Time horizon: [Years until mainstream impact]
Key signals: [3-5 specific, sourced signals]
Counter-signals: [Evidence this driver might stall or reverse]
Interaction effects: [Other drivers that amplify or dampen this one]
```

## Signal-to-Forecast Pipeline

```
Signals (many, specific, sourced)
    ↓ cluster by direction of change
Drivers (fewer, broader, named forces)
    ↓ combine interacting drivers
Forecasts (bold claims about what changes, grounded in drivers)
    ↓ develop into vivid narratives
Scenarios (internally consistent stories about life in that future)
    ↓ make tangible
Artifacts (concrete objects/content from that future world)
```

Each step up the pipeline is more interpretive and less empirical. That's fine — but always maintain the chain of evidence back down to signals.

## Source Discipline for Signal Gathering

### Source Hierarchy (prioritize in this order)
1. **Primary research:** Peer-reviewed journals, preprints (arXiv, SSRN, bioRxiv), patent filings, regulatory filings (SEC, FDA, EMA), court documents, government statistics
2. **Institutional sources:** Government data agencies (BLS, Census, Eurostat, ONS), central banks, WHO, UN, OECD, IEEE, NIST, standards bodies
3. **Grey literature with verifiable methodology:** Think tank reports (Brookings, RAND, Chatham House, McKinsey Global Institute), industry association data, foundation research with disclosed methods
4. **Quality journalism with named sources:** Financial Times, Reuters, Associated Press, Bloomberg, The Economist, domain-specific trade press (e.g., STAT News for biotech, The Information for tech)
5. **Company disclosures:** SEC filings, earnings call transcripts, technical engineering blogs with verifiable claims, open-source project activity
6. **Expert commentary with stated reasoning:** Named researchers or practitioners explaining their logic — not punditry, not prediction markets, not "top 10 trends" listicles

### Explicitly Deprioritized
- Marketing content, press releases, product announcements → useful as signals of *intent*, not evidence of *impact*
- Viral social media claims without primary sourcing
- Content farms, SEO-optimized summaries, AI-generated aggregation sites
- Sources with undisclosed conflicts of interest (vendor research about their own market)
- Prediction without reasoning ("X will happen by 2030" with no causal argument)
- Paywalled content that can't be verified or cited

### Signal Validation Rules
- Every signal cited must include a source that can be checked
- Distinguish "this is happening" (verifiable fact) from "this is being claimed" (attribution matters — name the claimant and their position)
- When a signal comes from a biased source, name the bias explicitly — the signal may still be real but the framing needs adjustment
- Prefer **convergent evidence:** a signal supported by 3 independent sources across different types is far stronger than one supported by a single loud source
- Date your signals — a signal from 5 years ago that hasn't accelerated is weaker than one from 6 months ago
- When using web search, construct queries targeting the source hierarchy, not generic queries that surface SEO content first
