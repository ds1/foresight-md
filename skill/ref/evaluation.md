# Evaluation & Quality Criteria Reference

## The Cascio Pentagon

Evaluate every major foresight output against five dimensions. Invented by Jamais Cascio at the IFTF Futurist Imagination Retreat.

### 1. Logical
- Is this internally consistent? Do the pieces fit together?
- Is the causal chain plausible? Can you trace signals → drivers → forecast → scenario?
- Does it produce an "a-ha" insight — a moment of recognition that something non-obvious is true?
- **Failure mode:** Internally contradictory, hand-waving causality, "and then magic happens" gaps.

### 2. Complex / Nuanced
- Does this account for many interacting factors, not just one trend?
- Does it include both positive and shadow imagination?
- Does it consider multiple stakeholder perspectives?
- Does it acknowledge what's uncertain vs. what's settled?
- **Failure mode:** Simplistic single-driver narrative, one-dimensional optimism or pessimism, ignoring counter-evidence.

### 3. Evocative
- Does this create an emotional response? Awe, wonder, hope, fear, urgency, compassion, curiosity?
- Can the reader *feel* what it would be like to live in this future?
- Are there concrete, sensory details — not just abstractions?
- **Failure mode:** Dry, clinical, abstract. Reads like a consultant's slide deck, not a lived future.

### 4. Provocative
- Does this upset assumptions? Create productive discomfort?
- Does it challenge how the world "should" work?
- Would it generate debate if shared with a diverse group?
- Does it make the reader reconsider something they took for granted?
- **Failure mode:** Safe, expected, confirms existing beliefs. Tells people what they already think.

### 5. Stimulating
- Does this spark further thinking? Does the reader's mind race with implications?
- Does it cascade into multiple domains — "if this is true, then what about...?"
- Does it generate more questions than it answers?
- **Test (from IFTF Retreat):** The stranger the ideas someone comes up with after hearing a forecast, the better the forecast.
- **Failure mode:** Neatly resolved, conversation-ending. Feels complete rather than opening.

### Scoring
After producing a major output, briefly self-assess:
- Which dimensions are strongest?
- Which are weakest?
- Offer to strengthen weak dimensions before moving on.

## Five Principles for Engaging Foresight Content

Apply these to all written output:

### 1. Don't Try to Predict
Frame forecasts as prompts to nudge the universe in a desired direction, not claims about what will happen. Language: "A plausible future in which..." not "What will happen is..."

### 2. Ground Everything in Signals
The future is not a distant event but the combined result of numerous interconnected micro-changes happening today. Every claim must trace back to observable present-day evidence. If you can't point to a signal, you're speculating without foundation.

### 3. Cut Everything That's Not Surprising
If something is obvious or expected, it's not worth including. The surprising elements are what make foresight engaging, memorable, and actionable. They spark curiosity and break mental models. Test: Would this make someone stop scrolling?

### 4. Set Audacious Visions
A forecast's power lies in opening minds to new possibilities and inspiring active shaping of the future. Bold beats bland. Provocation beats politeness. If no one pushes back, you weren't bold enough.

### 5. Anchor in Plausibility
Even wildly innovative scenarios must be plausible — meaning you can trace a credible causal path from today's signals through known drivers to the described future. Plausible scenarios invite people in. Implausible ones are dismissed. The sweet spot: surprising AND plausible.

## Plausibility Test

Before publishing any forecast, scenario, or artifact, verify:

1. **Signal chain:** Can I name 3+ present-day signals that point toward this future?
2. **Driver logic:** Can I name the macro-forces that would cause this transition?
3. **Precedent check:** Has something structurally similar happened before in a different domain or era?
4. **Counter-signal awareness:** Am I aware of signals that push *against* this future? Have I addressed them?
5. **Mechanism clarity:** Can I explain *how* we get from here to there, not just *that* we do?
6. **Time horizon sanity:** Is this achievable within the stated timeframe given historical rates of change?

If any of these fail, either strengthen the argument or reclassify from "plausible" to "possible/speculative" and label it as such.

## Output Self-Evaluation Template

Use after completing any major deliverable:

```
CASCIO PENTAGON ASSESSMENT
- Logical:      [Strong / Adequate / Weak] — [brief note]
- Complex:      [Strong / Adequate / Weak] — [brief note]
- Evocative:    [Strong / Adequate / Weak] — [brief note]
- Provocative:  [Strong / Adequate / Weak] — [brief note]
- Stimulating:  [Strong / Adequate / Weak] — [brief note]

PRINCIPLES CHECK
- [ ] Grounded in signals (not pure speculation)
- [ ] Contains surprising elements (not just expected trends)
- [ ] Audacious vision (bold enough to provoke)
- [ ] Plausible (traceable causal chain from today)
- [ ] Framed as possibility, not prediction

WEAKEST DIMENSION: [name]
SUGGESTED IMPROVEMENT: [specific action to strengthen it]
```
